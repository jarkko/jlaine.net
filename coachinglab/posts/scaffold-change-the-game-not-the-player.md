# Muuta peliä, älä pelaajaa / Change the game, not the player

Status: English draft/outline. Jarkko translates and voices for publication.

Working title candidates (Finnish):
- "Muuta peliä, älä pelaajaa"
- "Miksi käskeminen ei toimi — ja mitä tehdä sen sijaan"
- "Lopeta käskeminen, muuta peli"

---

## The problem you recognize

Open with a scene every beach volleyball coach has lived:

Your players serve. Both stay back. You've told them a hundred times: one of you needs to go to the net. They nod. Next rally, both back again. You tell them louder. It works for two points. Then both back.

This is the most common reaction: we tell them again, maybe more clearly, maybe with a cone, maybe with frustration. And it doesn't stick.

**The question this post asks:** What if the problem isn't that they don't listen — but that telling them is the wrong tool?

---

## Why they stay back (and why it's smart)

Don't frame both-back as a mistake. Frame it as an adaptation that makes sense *to them*:

- Staying deep feels safe — maximum reaction time, no one gets lobbed
- They don't yet trust that being at the net helps — they haven't *felt* the payoff
- They don't know who should go forward — and the silent question "you or me?" is easier to skip than to answer
- Most have played indoor, where "stay deep, react" is the norm — 2v2 beach demands a completely different positional logic
- In transition (after the first attack), it gets worse: neither player started in position, both can claim either role, and "both back" has zero coordination cost

Key point: this isn't a discipline problem or a listening problem. They're solving the game as they experience it. If we want different behavior, we need to change what they experience.

---

## What if we changed the game instead?

Introduce the idea without naming it yet:

Instead of telling players where to stand, what if we changed the rules so that having someone at the net *actually paid off* — visibly, immediately, in their own scoring?

Not a lecture. Not a diagram. A different game that makes the old habit expensive and the new habit rewarding.

### Try this: "Net touches pay"

2v2, normal play from serve. One rule change: if a net-side player on your team touches the ball before you win the rally (block, redirect, anything), the point is worth double.

That's it. No instruction about who goes forward. No blocking technique lesson. Just: net involvement pays.

What happens: within a few minutes, someone starts drifting forward. Not because you told them. Because the game told them.

### Then this: "Front space is expensive"

Keep 2v2. New rule: if the receiving team wins the rally with a ball landing in the front third, it's worth 2 points. Normal wins are 1.

Now both staying back creates an obvious invitation — the front court is undefended and the opponent knows it. The serving team has to solve that problem themselves.

Coach questions between games (not instructions):
- "What did the front space invite the attackers to do?"
- "When was it easier to win the point?"
- "What's hard about being at the net?"

### Then: "Pressure zone"

Mark a zone ~2-2.5m from the net. Serving team earns a bonus if, by the opponent's set, one player is in that zone and the team either wins or forces an easy ball.

"Pressure success" isn't just blocks — it's a hitter tipping instead of swinging freely, a high easy ball, a defender digging because the front player changed the angle.

Coach language:
- "Can one of you make the hitter solve a problem at the net?"
- "What changes for the defender when your partner is visible near the hitter?"

### Finally: fade the rules

Remove the bonuses. Play normal 2v2. Watch. Does someone go to the net without being told?

Often: yes. Not perfectly, not every time. But the behavior appeared without a single command. And because they discovered it themselves, it's theirs — it survives into the next session in a way that "go to the net" never did.

## What just happened

Now — after the reader has *felt* the approach work through a concrete example — name what's going on. Keep it plain:

We didn't teach a formation. We changed the game so that the useful formation became the obvious solution. The players organized themselves because the task rewarded it.

This works because the old habit (both back) was an adaptation to a game where net presence didn't visibly matter. When we made it matter — through scoring, through spatial rules, through consequences — the players adapted again. Different game, different solution.

The coach's job shifted from *telling* to *designing*. Instead of prescribing what to do, we designed a situation where the right thing to do was discoverable.

And here's the underlying mechanic the games surfaced: in beach volleyball, whoever ends up closer to the net after the team's last action becomes the blocker on the next play. After your serve, that's the non-server — they were already positioned up while you were deep recovering. After your attack, that's the attacker — they landed at the net coming down from their jump. The constraint games didn't invent this. They made it visible. The players discovered a real convention of the sport without anyone saying "now block."

## The coaching language shift

A brief table — "instead of this, try this":

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---|---|
| "Go to the net to block" | (Use the bonus rule. Let them choose.) |
| "You're standing too far back" | "Where did the ball want to go? Could you reach it from there?" |
| "Watch the hitter's shoulder" | "What was the hitter showing you before they hit?" |
| "Good block!" | "What did you see that told you to commit?" |
| "Both of you can't stay back" | "What happened when the front court was empty?" |
| "When she serves, you're the blocker" | "What was different when she served vs. when you did?" |

The pattern: replace commands with questions. Questions direct attention to the information that *guides* the decision, instead of removing the decision entirely.

## When it doesn't work (and that's fine)

Honest section. Not everything resolves in one session:

- Some players won't go forward in session one. That's normal — the attractor is strong. Repeat the constraint family across 3-5 sessions with variation: different partners, different scoring, different opponents.
- Transition is harder than serve-receive. In serve-receive there's a setup phase — who's serving determines who starts where. In transition, both players have moved during the rally and the question "who's up now?" has to get answered mid-action, often without a call. Don't expect it to solve itself from this session. That's a future post.
- This doesn't teach blocking *technique* — hand shape, timing, reading the setter. That's separate work. This session teaches *why* and *when* to be at the net. Technique follows when they want to be there.
- And sometimes both staying back IS the right answer — weak attack, scramble play, emergency. The goal is players who choose, not players who obey a rule.

## Footnote: this has a name

For coaches who want to dig deeper:

The approach in this post draws from something called the **constraints-led approach** (CLA) in coaching science. The idea: instead of prescribing technique or tactics, the coach manipulates the *constraints* of the task (rules, scoring, space, equipment) so that useful behaviors emerge from the players themselves.

It's part of a broader framework called **nonlinear pedagogy** — the study of how coordination patterns self-organize when the environment changes, rather than being installed by instruction.

If you want to read more:
- Renshaw, Davids, Newcombe & Roberts — *The Constraints-Led Approach* (Routledge, 2019) — the most practical book on this
- John Kessel's "Stop Teaching" series (USA Volleyball) — a practitioner take on the same ideas
- Wulf, G. (2013) — research on why external-focus cues (questions about the ball/target/effect) produce better learning than internal-focus cues (instructions about the body)
