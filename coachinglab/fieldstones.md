# Fieldstones

Raw material for future content. Stories, observations, anecdotes, vignettes from coaching practice. Following Gerald Weinberg's fieldstone method (*Weinberg on Writing: The Fieldstone Method*, 2005).

## The metaphor

New England farmers built walls from stones they unearthed while plowing. They gathered stones in heaps as the fields produced them, and only later assembled walls from the pile. Stones came from doing the work, not from going hunting for stones.

The writing parallel:

- Gather "stones" (ideas, anecdotes, observations) as they surface in daily life
- Don't filter, don't worry yet about how they'll be used
- Later, browse the pile, find stones that fit, assemble into a wall (post, chapter, essay)
- The collection is the persistent asset; individual writings are derivable from it

Capture comes from doing the coaching, not from sitting down to "find content." Connection happens later.

## Format

Each stone goes here as it occurs. Append to the end. Each stone needs: a date and the stone itself. Tags in brackets optional but useful for later browsing.

Example:

> ### 2026-05-26
> At U14 girls practice, I yelled "go to the net!" and one of them stepped *back* instead of forward. Two practices later, with the +2 net-touch bonus in place and zero verbal direction from me, she went up on her own. [tags: blocking, u14, attractor, instruction-paradox]

Guidelines:

- **Concrete.** Specific moment, specific observation. A name (or "one of the U14s"), a place, a sentence said, a movement seen.
- **Don't generalize at capture time.** Generalization is part of wall-building, not stone-gathering. If a stone wants to be a lesson, let it. If it wants to stay a story, leave it as one.
- **Either language.** Write in whatever language the moment surfaced in. Mixing Finnish and English here is fine — this is the dump, not the published wall.
- **Naming people.** Real names are fine in stones (helps you recall context later). If a person shouldn't appear in any published version, flag the heading with `[DO NOT NAME IN PUBLIC]` and add tag `do-not-name`. Anonymization happens at wall-building, not at capture.
- **Trust the pile.** A stone that looks small or uninteresting now might be the key piece of a wall in six months.

### 2026-05-26 [theme]

In 2v2 beach (youth + recreational adult), the default is for both players to stay back after their own serve rather than have one at the net for blocking. We want them playing the "real" beach game where one blocks (even if they peel off after the set) and the other defends. Explicit instruction doesn't stick — they revert when the rule disappears. Even at the advanced level, the pattern works after a serve but collapses in transition, especially without a dedicated blocker — *role ambiguity under time pressure* defaults the system to the safer "both back."

This is the seed observation for the block-defense post arc. The Kristiansson "perception not skill" stone, the transition-collapse note from advanced play, the +1 net-touch constraint design, and any future practice observations on this all cluster here. [tags: theme, beach, blocking, attractor, youth, transition, post-arc-seed]

### 2026-05-26

Indoor junior volleyball: after a block, hitters often aren't available for the setter on the next play because they don't peel off from the net, or peel off too slowly. They need to transition immediately into their approach to be a viable attack option — but the "block-done" state pulls them to watch-and-stand rather than to transition. Setter ends up with fewer options. [tags: indoor, junior, blocking, transition, peel-off, attacking, setter-availability, attractor]

### 2026-05-28 [insight]

Why staying back "feels right" to the kids even when it's costing them: any *single* rally often doesn't punish both-back. The weak hitter mishits, the loose set goes nowhere, the opponents make their own error. So the kids' local picture — *this* rally, *this* hitter, *this* set — tells them staying back was fine. They're solving the visible problem correctly.

What the local picture hides is the aggregate:

- Overpasses with nobody up there to put away
- Sets that drift over the net and need to be handled there
- Sets so tight a low block beats no block
- The presence effect — just having a body up changes the hitter's swing, the setter's set, the comfort of the whole opposing system, with no touch required

Over enough rallies, the un-blocked hitter has freedom that gradually wins. But the kid can't see that pattern in any single moment. They feel safe rally-by-rally and stay deep.

This is precisely what a well-designed constraint does: it makes the aggregate cost local. A scoring rule that doubles points for net-side touches converts an invisible long-run truth into a visible per-rally consequence. The kid doesn't have to track patterns across a session in their head — the game does it for them, rally by rally.

This generalises beyond block/defense. Many "stuck" coaching problems are aggregate-cost problems that the player's local picture hides. The pedagogical move is the same: design a constraint that makes the aggregate cost local. [tags: insight, pedagogy, constraints, aggregation, invisible-cost, both-back, beach, scoring-design, generalisable]
