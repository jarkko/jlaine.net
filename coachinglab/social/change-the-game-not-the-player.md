## Threads / Mastodon — 1/4

A scenario every beach volleyball coach will recognise: two juniors who keep both staying back, even when one of them needs to be at the net. Four weeks of telling them — calmly, then with diagrams in the sand, then with what you might call professional irritation. None of it sticks.

## Threads / Mastodon — 2/4

Eventually you have to admit something less flattering. The kids are doing the thing that makes sense from where they stand. Both back is the safer choice. And in any single rally, the punishment for staying back doesn't come labelled — the opponent picked a corner, the hitter timed it well, the set drifted over. Each loss has its own visible reason, and none of them say "you should have been at the net."

## Threads / Mastodon — 3/4

So you stop telling and design the game instead. A scoring tweak that pays for net touches. A rule that makes the empty front court actively expensive. By the third or fourth rally, kids who'd been told a hundred times to go up start going up — because the game rewards it, not because someone yelled.
